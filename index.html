    TRIUMVIRATES
    OF ANCIENT ROME

    A brief and simplified approach to the two triumvirates of ancient rome.

First Triumvirate

The First Triumvirate of ancient Rome was an uneasy alliance between the three great romans Gaius Julius Caesar, Gnaius Pompeius Magnus and Marcus Lucinius Crassus which, from 60 BCE until 53 BCE, dominated the politics of the Roman Republic.

The Republic was in dire straits. Roman political order was in chaos. There was street violence and rioting. To some the Roman citizenry was falling victim to moral decay, in the midst of this chaos three men seized the opportunity for personal gain, forming an alliance or triumvirate that would eventually transform the government.

The three men who would change the face of Roman politics were Gnaius Pompeius Magnus, Marcus Lucinius Crassus, and Gaius Julius Caesar. Each had his own personal reason for joining together, realizing that he could not achieve it alone. While each had attained personal success, he wanted even more gloria and dignitas.
In60 BCE the three men combined their resources, set aside their personal differences (Crassus, although one of the wealthiest men in Rome, actually despised Pompey) and seized control of the state; however, despite good intentions and personal achievements aside, the union was tenuous at best.

Marcus Lucinius Crassus

Marcus Licinius Crassus (115-53 BCE) was perhaps the richest man in Roman history and in his eventful life he experienced both great successes and severe disappointments. His vast wealth and sharp political skills brought him two consulships and the kind of influence enjoyed only by a true heavyweight of Roman politics. A mentor to Julius Caesar in his early career, Crassus would rise to the very top of state affairs but his long search for a military triumph to match his great rival Pompey would, ultimately, bring about his downfall. During the Spartacus revolt in 73 BCE, Crassus was elected praetor and was sent into battle against Spartacus by the Senate. In 71 BCE Crassus effectively ended the Spartacus Uprising and intending to leave a warning to anyone thinking of rebelling against Rome in the future, Crassus ordered the crucifixion of 600 slaves on the road to Rome. The six thousand captured slaves were crucified along the Via Appia by Crassus' orders. At his command, their bodies were not taken down afterwards, but remained rotting along Rome's principal route to the south. This was intended as an abject lesson to anyone who might think of rebelling against Rome in the future, particularly of slave insurrections against their owners and masters, the Roman citizens. Crassus effectively ended the Third Servile War in 71 BC. Crassus was the son of Publius Licinius Crassus, who was consul in 97 BCE and a commander in Iberia, even gaining a triumph for his victories in Lusitania in 93 BCE. In 87 BCE, on the losing side against the forces of Gaius Marius and Cornelius Cinna, he committed suicide and the young Crassus fled to Spain. Crassus died in his attempt to conquer Parthia, in what was his last and fateful throw of the political dice.

Gnaius Pompeius Magnus

Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus (106-48 BCE), also known as Pompeu the Great, was a military leader and politician during the fall of the Roman Republic.
Pompey's military career started in the Social Wars (91-89 BCE) when he served under his father's, Gnaeus Pompeius Strabo, army at Asculum (89 BCE). In 77 BCE Pompey was sent pro consule in order to assist in the struggle against Sertorius in Spain, returning from Spain in 71 BCE. During the course of his return, Pompey defeated the scattered and fleeing troops of Spartacus' army. With this, Pompey saw the opportunity to claim that he defeated the revolt of Spartacus, a feat accomplished by Crassus, in order to receive the credit and glory.
The death of Julia in 54 BCE and the death of Crassus in 53 BCE, the tension between Julius Caesar and Pompey the Great intensified and eventually led to civil war, which resulted in the death of Pompey at Alexandria, Egypt, in 48 BCE.

Gaius Julius Caesar

Gaius Julius Caesar (100-44 BCE) son of Gaius Julius Caesar, a Praetor who governed the province of Asia, and Aurelia Cotta, Both advocated the democratization of government and more rights for the lower class, as opposed to favoring the upper classes and the nobility. Which resulted in large numbers of enemies.
When Sulla declared himself dictator of Rome, he began a systematic purge of his enemies and particularly those who espoused the Populare ideology. Cesar was targeted and had to flee Rome. In order to support the family, Caesar joined the army, and after Sulla's death, he returned to Rome and became an orator. In 62 BCE he was elected Propraetor(governor) of Hispania where defeated the warring rival tribes, brought stability to the region being awarded a consulship by the senate.
In 52 BCE Caesar defeated the Gallic leader Vercingetorix and completed the conquest of Gaul, becoming sovereign of the province of Gaul. In 49 BC, Caesar crossed the Rubicon River with his legions and marched on Rome in 49 BC, being an act of war against Rome, realizing the beginning of the civil war between Caesar and Pompey, who marched to Egypt in pursuit of Pompey. After Pompey's death, Caesar proclaimed martial law in Egypt, deposed the co-regent, Ptolemy XIII, and allied himself with Cleopatra, starting the war between Caesar's legions and the Egyptian army, which was defeated by the forces of Caesar in 47 B.C.
On March 15, 44 BCE, Caesar was assassinated by the senators in the portico of the basilica of Pompey the Great.

SECOND TRIUMVIRATE

The Second Triumvirate was a political association of convenience between three of Rome's most powerful figures: Marcus Antonius, Lepidus, and Octavian in the 1st century BCE.

After the assassination of Julius Caesar, the assassins believed that his death would restore balance to the republic and the Roman spirit. Marcus Junius Brutus, one of the main conspirators for the assassination, announced to the public about the death of Julius Caesar, and noticed that the people of Rome became enraged and hostile, forcing the conspirators to flee Rome.

Following the assassination of Julius Caesar the three vowed revenge on his killers and attempted to stabilise the Roman Republic in what would turn out to be its death throes. However, the egos of these three men would soon clash, they would eventually meet in battle and, as a result, a single emperor would emerge.

Marcus Antonius

Marcus Antonious (83-30 BC) was a Roman general, statesman and right-hand man of Julius Caesar. As one of the members of the second triumvirate, Marcus ruled without harmony with the other members, Octavianus Caesar and Marcus Lepidus. Having quickly risen through the ranks of Gabinus, Antonius was promoted and summoned by Julius Caesar for the Gaul expedition in 54 BCE. Marcus Antonius proved to be a brilliant commander, but he had addictions to lust, drink and sex, resulting in the alienation of Caesar and the other officers. After Caesar's conquest of Gaul, in which Antonius was instrumental, in 50 BCE, Caesar supported Antonious in his election as tribune. After Caesar gained control of Rome, Caesar left the administration of Rome under the power of Marcus Antonious, who proved to have little ability as a politician. After Caesar's victory over Pompey in Greece, Marcus returned to rule Rome. Conducting an incompetent administration, he was removed from the government and succeeded by Lepidus. In 44 BC, after Caesar's assassination, Octavian's arrival and legal claim as Caesar's heir. Antonius and Octavius were constantly at odds. In a truce, Antony, Octavian, and Lepidus formed the Second Triumvirate in 43 BC and agreed to divide Rome's estates between them to rule jointly. Lepidus was given Africa to rule, Octavian the west from Rome, and Antony the east. In 31 BCE, the army of Antonius and Cleopatra faced the army of Octavius at the Battle of Actium (31 BCE) with Octavius as victorious, profoundly impacting Antonius' forces and in 30 BCE upon receiving a false rumor of the death of Cleopatra, Marcus antonious committed suicide.

Marcus Aemilius Lepidus

Marcus Aemilius Lepidus (89-12 BCE) was a Roman statesman and one of the triumvirs who ruled Rome after 43 BCE. Lepidus, who sided with Julius Caesar during the civil war between Caesar and Pompey, received the following titles: praetor in 49 BCE, governors of Spain in 48 and 47 BCE, and consul in 46 BCE. In 45 BCE, trusted by Julius Caesar, he became his army's magister equitum. After the assassination of Julio Cesar, Lepido allied with Marco Antonio against the conspirators, having received the title of pontifex maximus. After being considered an enemy of Rome by the Senate, Lepidus met with Marco Antonio and Octavio Augustus and formed the second triumvirate. During the Triumvirate, Lepidus became sovereign of Spain and southern Gaul, and gradually lost his power and sovereignty to Antonio and Octavius, resulting in a failed attempt at rebellion, from Sicily, against Octavius in 36 BCE which forced him to withdraw from public life in Rome.

Gaius Julius Caesar Octavianus Augustus

Octavian Augustus Caesar (63-14 BC) was the first and greatest Roman emperor. After the assassination of Julius Caesar in 44 BCE, Octavius allied with Marcus Antonius and Lepidus formed the Second Triumvirate in 43 BCE. After the start of the triumvirate, Octavius began a systematic purge of any political rivals and supporters of Caesar assassins. At the Battle of Phillipi in 42 BC, the forces of Brutus and Cassius were defeated by those of the Second Triumvirate, and in 36 BC, Octavian and Lepidus fought Sextus Pompeus, son of Pompeus the Great, and were successful. Lepidus tried to use the glory of victory to confront Octavius politically and militarily, demanding control of Sicily, however, Octavius bought Lepidus' troops and removed him from public life. In 34 BC the Senate revoked Antonius consulship and declared war on Cleopatra VII. At the Battle of Actium in 31 BCE, Augustus army defeated the combined armies of Antonius and Cleopatra, and after the loss of Alexandria, Antonius and Cleopatra killed each other. Augustus had Caesarion and Antonius eldest son executed for being considered a threat to Rome. The second triumvirate came to an end and Augustus became supreme ruler of Rome. Popular with his armies, the title of Augustus solidified his power in the provinces as Imperator. In 19 BCE he become the first Emperor of Rome and in 2 BCE was declared Pater Patriae, father of his country. The era of Augustus reign was a golden age in every respect, he carried out Julius Caesar plans and then proceeded with his own grandiose projects, restored and maintained the Pax Romana, made the economy, arts and agriculture flourish. He passed many radical reforms, and Augustus himself adhered so strictly to his laws that he banished his own daughter Julia and granddaughter for adultery. Augustus died at Nola in 14 BCE. His last official words were: “I found Rome a city of clay, but I left it a city of marble”, which aptly describes Augustus' achievements during his reign as emperor. Augustus' death was mourned and he was proclaimed a god among the hosts of the Roman pantheon.

Curiosities

Some curious facts about Rome or important figures during the first and second triumvirate.

Crassus, Pompeu and The Uprising of Spartacus

In 70 BCE Spartacus, a Thracian gladiator, initiated the slave rebellion in Rome, having gathered an army numbering 120,000. After some defeats by Rome, the senate commissioned Crassus to quell Spartacus' rebellion. In 69 BCE, Crassus cornered Spartacus and his army in Lucania and defeated them, putting down the rebellion completely. However, Pompey the Great, a rival of Crassus who played no significant part in the repression, received most of the honor and triumph when he returned to Rome.

Marcus Vipsanius Agrippa (64-12 BCE)

Agrippa was Augustus Caesar's most trusted and unswervingly loyal general and his right-hand man in administering the city of Rome. He was one of the most skilled military commanders in Roman history, a talented engineer, architect and administrator, instrumental in consolidating the Roman Empire. One of his greatest achievements was the victory against Marcus Antonius and Cleopatra VII, which consolidated Augustus Caesar as the only one in power in Rome.

August Month

Augustus Caesar received two honors. The title of Augustus solidified his power in the provinces as Imperator, which he derived from the title of Emperor. The month of August was named in his honor.
